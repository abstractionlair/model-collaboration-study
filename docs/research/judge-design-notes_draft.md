# LLM-as-Judge Design Notes

**STATUS: DRAFT — Incomplete.** These notes were extracted from exploratory
conversations and have not been formally reviewed or validated. They will need

## Why Frontier-as-Judge Is Plausible Here

The experimental design deliberately calibrates tasks to be moderately difficult
for small subject models (~40-60% one-shot success) but easy for frontier
judges. This avoids the main failure mode: a 2025 study found that without a
correct reference, judge agreement with humans drops sharply on questions the
judge itself cannot answer. **GPT-4o's pairwise agreement dropped from 0.86 to
0.16** on questions it couldn't answer when using its own answer as reference,
while human-written references substantially improved agreement.

By construction, the tasks should be comfortably inside the frontier judge's
competence envelope. The core condition to verify is not the small models'
success rate by itself, but that the judge can itself solve or correctly assess
these items at a very high rate on a labeled calibration slice.

## Known Biases

- **Self-preference / family bias:** Judges tend to favor outputs stylistically
  closer to themselves. One paper found significant self-preference bias in
  GPT-4-class judging. Mitigation: cross-family judging (leave-one-family-out
  rule where OpenAI doesn't judge GPT outputs, etc.), or a small judging panel
  from different families.
- **Position bias:** Well-established problem. Mitigation: randomize answer
  order, judge both orders when feasible.
- **Verbosity bias:** Well-established. Longer answers tend to score higher
  regardless of quality.
- **Capability mismatch:** If the judge cannot solve or verify the underlying
  task, it may confidently endorse the wrong answer unless given a correct
  reference.


## Judge Information Regimes

One useful framing from the exploratory conversations is that "judge
design" is not just model choice. What the judge is allowed to see is
itself an experimental variable. Candidate regimes:

- **Final answer only:** Cleanest and least confounded, but may hide useful
  evidence of reasoning quality or failure modes.
- **Final answer + process traces:** May improve some judgments, but also
  introduces extra surface area for verbosity, persuasion, and style bias.
- **Reference-guided judging:** Strong candidate default for open-ended
  tasks, especially when the reference is generated by a stronger model and
  kept separate from the compared answers.
- **Execution-augmented judging:** Best regime for coding and tool use when
  tests, executability, or harness outcomes are available.

The notes from the conversations lean toward treating this as something to
record explicitly and possibly ablate, rather than an invisible prompt
choice.

## Judging Approaches

- **Pairwise comparison** is generally more stable and better aligned with human
  preferences than absolute/pointwise scoring. Recent work found materially
  better head-to-head decision utility than pointwise scores. But pairwise can
  be more vulnerable to distractor features and biasing artifacts.
  Recommendation: pairwise for winner selection, rubric scores for analysis.
- **Rubric-based judging** outperforms vague "which is better?" prompts.
  Anthropic's production LLM judging used explicit dimensions: factual accuracy,
  citation accuracy, completeness, source quality, tool efficiency. The
  conversations recommended similar structured rubrics for this project.
- **Reference-guided judging:** A 2026 study across 11 judges and 5 benchmarks
  found high-quality references substantially improved judge accuracy.
  References from a stronger model gave a **6.8% absolute improvement** over
  reference-free baseline. For this project's open-ended tasks, have the
  frontier model generate a strong reference, then judge pipeline output
  relative to that reference.

## Project-Specific Hypotheses

### "Flag don't Explain"
A unique thread in this research is the hypothesis that **explanation-heavy critique can be a source of noise**, especially for smaller models. Preliminary observations suggest that lightweight models may correctly "smell" or flag a problem but then reason themselves into a false negative when forced to generate a long rationale. Restricting reviewers to structured flags (identifying the *existence* and *type* of an error) might preserve more signal than requiring a full explanation.
- **Judge panels / juries:** The PoLL paper reported better correlation with
  humans than a single GPT-4 judge while being much cheaper. Multiple judges
  from different families reduce intra-model bias.

## Practical Judge Protocol

The conversations converged on this layered approach:

1. **Primary frontier judge** for routine pairwise comparisons.
2. **Second judge** only on close calls or disagreements.
3. **Human review** on a sampled holdout and on the most consequential failures.

For the full protocol:

- Blind model identities before judging.
- Randomize answer order and judge both orders when feasible.
- Prefer pairwise comparisons over raw 1-10 scores.
- Use structured rubrics with task-relevant dimensions.
- For coding/tool use, combine model judging with executable checks.
- Keep a human-audited holdout to calibrate the judge and catch drift.

Useful escalation triggers to record explicitly:

- Judge reports low confidence.
- Pairwise margin is small or unstable across answer order.
- Multiple judges disagree.
- Execution result and judge verdict conflict.
- A result is surprising enough to materially change the study conclusion.

## Task-Type-Specific Judging

- **Coding / tool use:** Let execution dominate. Tests passing, BFCL
  executability, patch acceptance by harness. Use frontier judge only where
  execution is unavailable or incomplete (explanation quality, tradeoff
  handling, patch plausibility before test execution).
- **Open-ended helpfulness:** Automatic judging acceptable as a screen, but
  manually review a sample. Reference-guided judging most valuable here.
- **Reasoning tasks:** Executable verification preferred where possible; model
  judge for process quality.

## Calibration

Build difficulty bands, not just a single target:

- 30-40% one-shot success (harder for subjects)
- 45-55% one-shot success (middle zone)
- 60-70% one-shot success (easier for subjects)

This lets the study see whether multi-step pipelines help mainly near the
decision boundary or more broadly. The key empirical check: verify on a labeled
calibration slice that the judge can itself solve or correctly assess these
items at a very high rate.


## Operational Guardrails

- Pin judge model versions where possible so the evaluation target does not
  drift during the study.
- Randomize answer order systematically rather than ad hoc.
- Keep the judged outputs on a common-denominator interface when comparing
  subject models, or the judge may end up rewarding vendor-specific tool
  affordances instead of protocol quality.
- Separate routine high-throughput judging from a smaller audited slice used
  to monitor drift, bias, and calibration over time.

## Caveats

- "Easy for frontier models" is not the same as "judge-perfect." A task can be
  easy to solve but awkward to grade due to ambiguity, multiple valid answers,
  style differences, or hidden constraints.
- LLM-as-judge does not remove the need for human oversight. Reliable
  LLM-as-judge requires careful design and standardization, not just a strong
  model.
- Anthropic reported that human testing caught important failures their
  automated judge missed even in their own production evaluation.
